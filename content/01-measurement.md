Before we get to rockets, black holes, or any of the cool stuff, we need to talk
about something more fundamental: how do you even measure anything?

If I said an atom is tiny and the Milky Way is huge — sure, but how tiny? How
huge? And compared to what?

That's what Chapter 1 is really about. Not formulas. Not calculations. Just: how
do we agree on what things are, so that a scientist in Nigeria and a scientist in
Japan are talking about the exact same thing?

[VISUAL: fig-1]

## The problem of measurement

Physics is about measuring the world. Length, time, mass, temperature, pressure —
all of it needs measuring. And to measure something you need two things: a unit
and a standard.

The unit is just the name — like "meter." The standard is the real-world thing
that defines exactly what one of those units looks like.

[VISUAL: fig-2]

Here's the smart move physicists made: you can't define a standard for every
physical quantity — that would be chaos. So they picked a small number of base
quantities — length, time, mass — defined standards for just those, and derived
everything else from them.

Speed? Length divided by time. Power? Mass times length squared divided by time
cubed. It all builds up from those three.

[VISUAL: fig-3]

## The SI system

This system has an official name: the International System of Units, or SI, from
the French *Système International*. You probably know it as the metric system.

[VISUAL: fig-4]

The three base units we care about right now:

- Meter (m) — length
- Second (s) — time
- Kilogram (kg) — mass

Simple enough. But here's the interesting part: how do you define these so they
are both permanent and accessible to anyone, anywhere?

[VISUAL: fig-5]

## Defining the meter

Back in 1792, France was having its revolution and decided to revolutionize
measurement too. They defined the meter as one ten-millionth of the distance from
the North Pole to the equator.

[VISUAL: fig-6]

Cool idea — but you can't carry the equator around in your pocket. So they
switched to a physical object: a platinum-iridium bar kept in Paris. One meter
was the distance between two tiny scratches on that bar.

[VISUAL: fig-7]

That worked for a while. But scratches wear down and aren't perfectly precise,
and science kept needing more accuracy. By 1960 the meter was redefined using the
wavelength of light — specifically orange-red light from krypton-86 atoms,
exactly 1,650,763.73 wavelengths of it.

[VISUAL: fig-8]

Then in 1983 they went even further. The meter is now the distance light travels
in a vacuum in exactly $1/299{,}792{,}458$ of a second.

[VISUAL: fig-9]

In other words, the speed of light is now *defined* to be exactly 299,792,458
meters per second. Not measured — defined. The meter adjusts to make that true.
That's a little mind-bending, but it's incredibly stable. Light doesn't change.
Ever.

## Defining the second

Time gets a similar treatment. A second used to be $1/86{,}400$ of a day — 24
hours, each 60 minutes, each 60 seconds.

But Earth's rotation isn't perfectly constant.

[VISUAL: fig-10]

That's real data from the textbook: tiny variations of a few milliseconds in the
length of the day, caused by tidal forces from the Moon. The Earth wobbles.
Clocks don't.

So now one second is defined as the time it takes a cesium-133 atom to oscillate
9,192,631,770 times — the frequency of a specific wavelength it emits.

[VISUAL: fig-11]

Atomic clocks built on this are so precise that two of them would have to run for
6,000 years before drifting apart by even one second.

[VISUAL: fig-12]

## Defining the kilogram

Now mass — and this one is a little awkward. For a long time, until very
recently, the standard kilogram was literally a cylinder of platinum and iridium
sitting in a vault outside Paris.

[VISUAL: fig-13]

Every country's mass measurements traced back to that one object. If it gained a
dust particle, every scale on Earth was technically off.

For atomic-scale measurements, scientists use a different unit: the atomic mass
unit, defined by the carbon-12 atom, which is assigned exactly 12 atomic mass

## Talking about very big and very small things

One more practical tool: prefixes. Physics spans from the radius of a proton
(about $10^{-15}$ m) to the distance to the first galaxies (around
$2 \times 10^{26}$ m).

Writing all those zeros is painful, so we use powers of ten with a prefix
attached to the unit.

[VISUAL: fig-16]

A nanometer is $10^{-9}$ meters. A gigawatt is $10^{9}$ watts. A millisecond is
$10^{-3}$ seconds. You already use these — megabyte, kilometer, centimeter.
They're all shorthand for powers of ten.

## Significant figures

Last idea: significant figures. Your measurement is never perfectly precise, and
when you calculate with measurements you can't pretend your answer is more
precise than your inputs.

[VISUAL: fig-17]

If your data has two significant figures, your answer should have two. The extra
digits on your calculator are noise that looks like information.

There's a nice distinction here: significant figures aren't the same as decimal
places. The numbers 35.6 mm, 3.56 m, and 0.00356 m all have three significant
figures — but one, two, and five decimal places respectively.

[VISUAL: fig-18]

Significant figures are about certainty, not scale.

## The big takeaway

So what's the big takeaway from Chapter 1?

Physics isn't just equations. It starts with a question that sounds almost
philosophical: what does it mean to measure something? The answer turns out to
require international agreement, atomic vibrations, the speed of light, and a
metal cylinder in a French vault.

Before you can do any physics, you decide what the irreducible things are that
you'll build everything else from. For mechanics, that's length, time, and mass.
Everything else — speed, energy, force — is built on top of those three.

[VISUAL: fig-19]

That's the foundation. Next chapter, we start moving things around in space.
